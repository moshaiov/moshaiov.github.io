nilpotent operators

claim let $T\in\End(V)$, $n=\dim V$. then $\{v\mid \exists k : T^kv=0\}=\ker T^n$. in particular, $T$ is nilpotent iff $T^n=0$.

proof
consider the chain $\{0\}=\ker T^0\sub \ker T\sub \ker T^2\sub\dots\sub\ker T^n\sub \ker T^{n+1}\sub \dots$ . it must stabilize. namely, let $m$ be the smallest solution for $\ker T^k=\ker T^{k+1}$. then $m\le n$ and since $T^{m+1}x=0\implies T^mx=0$, we have $\ker T^m=\ker T^{m+1}=\ker T^{m+2}=\ker T^{m+3}=\dots$ . ergo $\bigcup_{k\ge0}\ker T^k=\ker T^m=\ker T^n$. ▨


theorem [Jordan] the following equivalent statements hold true for any nilpotent operator $T\in\End V$.
$\cdot$ $V$ may be decomposed as $V=\bigoplus_{j=1}^r V_j$ where $T$ acts as a nilpotent shift on each $V_j$.
$\cdot$ $T$ has a block diagonal matrix form where each block has the form $ \tiny{\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & & & \\ & 0 & 1 & & \\ & & \ddots & 1 & \\ & & & 0 & 1 \\ & & & & 0 \end{bmatrix}}$
$\cdot$ there exists $v_1,\dots,v_r\in V$ such that $\O_{v_1}\sqcup\dots\sqcup\O_{v_r}$ is a basis of $V$, where $\O_v=\{v,Tv,T^2v,\dots\}\setminus\{0\}$.

proof
by induction on $\dim V$. base case : a nilpotent $1\times 1$ matrix is $[0]$.
step : let $W=\img T$. if $W=0$ then $T=0$ and we're done. otherwise consider $T|_W\in\End W$. let $(w_1,\dots,T^{f_1}w_1,\dots,w_k,\dots,T^{f_k}w_k)$ be an orbital union basis of $W$.
fix $Tv_j=w_j$ ($j\in[k]$) as well as $u_1,\dots,u_r$ completing $T^{f_1}w_1,\dots,T^{f_k}w_k$ to a basis of $\ker T$.
the orbital union $(v_1,w_1,\dots,T^{f_1}w_1,\dots,v_k,w_k,\dots,T^{f_k}w_k,u_1,\dots,u_r)$ is a list of $\dim V$ linearly independent vectors, i.e. a basis. ▨

over $\huge{\R}$ and $\huge{\C}$

claim for $A\in\C^{n\times n}$ we have $\det e^A=e^{\tr A}$

proof
the result is clear for diagonal matrices $D$. if $A=PDP\inv$ is diagonalizable, then $e^A=Pe^DP\inv$ so $\det e^A=e^{\tr D}=e^{\tr A}$ holds too. finally, the result for $A\in\C^{n\times n}$ general follows by the denseness of the diagonalizable matrices. ▨

Feynman method

example 1 we'll evaluate $\disp \int_0^1\frac{x^3-x^2}{\ln x}\d x$.

solution
extending the problem, let $f(t)=\disp\int_0^1\frac{x^t-x^2}{\ln(x)}\d x$, convergent for $t>-1$. Leibniz yields $f'(t)=\disp \int_0^1 x^t\d x=\dfrac{1}{t+1}$. thus $f(t)=\ln(t+1)+C$. the value of $C=-\ln 3$ is given by $f(2)=0$. thus $f(3)=\ln(4/3)$ is the value we wanted. ▨


example 2 we'll evaluate the Fourier transform of the Gaussian, namely $\disp f(\xi)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}e^{-x^2}e^{-2\pi i\xi x}\d x$.

solution
calling the integrand $\Psi$, Leibniz yields $\disp f'(\xi)= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \partial_\xi\Psi\d x$. but $\partial_\xi\Psi=-2\pi ix\Psi=\pi i(\partial_x \Psi +2\pi i\xi \Psi)$ gives $f'(\xi)=-2\pi^2\xi f(\xi)$. we solve this linear ODE by $f(\xi)=C e^{-\pi^2\xi^2}$. finally, the well known $f(0)=\sqrt{\pi}$ yields $$\disp\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}e^{-x^2}e^{-2\pi i\xi x}\d x=\sqrt{\pi}e^{-\pi^2\xi^2}$$ ▨

combinatorial methods

claim Sperner's lemma implies Brouwer's fixed point theorem

proof
suppose for the sake of contradiction $\Delta^d\arr{f}\Delta^d$ is continuous with no fixed points. then $\forall x\in\Delta^d\exists i\st x_i > f(x)_i$. we pick any such $i$ and color $x$ using it. this yields a Sperner coloring (on any triangulation) of $\Delta^d$. for each $n$ let $\TT_n$ be a triangulation of $\Delta^n$ with diameter less than $1/n$. let $T_n\in\TT_n$ be a Sperner simplex, and let $x^0_n,\dots,x^d_n$ be its vertices, with $x^i_n$ colored $i$, and any pair distanced at most $1/n$ apart. let $x\in\Delta^d$ be an accumulation point of $x^0_n$. ergo $x$ is also an accumulation point of $x^i_n$ for each $i$. it follows that $f(x)_i\le x_i$ for all $i$. since $1=\sum x_i=\sum f(x)_i$, it follows that $x$ is a fixed point of $f$. ▨

winding number

prototypical example $\deg(t\mapsto e^{2\pi int})=n$

claim let $\gamma:[0,1]\to\C^\times$ be a path. there exists a continuous map $\xi:[0,1]\to\C$ s.t. $\gamma=e^\xi$.
moreover, the lift $\xi$ is unique up to additions of integer multiples of $2\pi i$.

proof
[existence] since a continuous logarithm exists on $\R_{>0}$, we may assume $\gamma:[0,1]\to\T$. using uniform continuity, and the existence of a continuous argument function on any (proper) arc of $\T$, we may fix $N$ s.t. $\forall j\in[N]$ $\exists [\frac{j-1}{N},\frac{j}{N}]\arr{\xi_j}\R$ with $\gamma|_{[\frac{j-1}{N},\frac{j}{N}]}=e^{i\xi_j}$. inductively, we may add integer multiples of $2\pi$ to $\xi_{j+1}$ and make $\xi_j,\xi_{j+1}$ agree at $\frac{j}{N}$. this results in a continuous lift $\gamma=e^{i\xi}$.
[uniqueness] $e^\xi=e^{\xi'}$ implies the path $\xi-\xi'$ takes values in $2\pi i\Z$, and ergo is constant. ▨


definition given a path $\gamma:[0,1]\to\C^\times$, its degree is $\dfrac{\xi(1)-\xi(0)}{2\pi i}$, where $\xi$ is (any) lift of $\gamma$.
in particular, $\gamma$ is closed (i.e. a loop) iff $\deg(\gamma)\in\Z$.

observation $\deg(\gamma_1\gamma_2)=\deg(\gamma_1)+\deg(\gamma_2)$.

exercise if the loop $\gamma:[0,1]\to\C\setminus \R_{\ge0}$ then $\deg(\gamma)=0$.

corollary [dog walking] if the loops $\gamma,\gamma':[0,1]\to\C^\times$ are such that $|\gamma-\gamma'|<|\gamma|$ then $\deg(\gamma')=\deg(\gamma)$.

corollary let $f\in\C[z]$ have degree $d$. if $R$ is sufficiently large then $\deg(t\mapsto f(Re^{2\pi i t}))=d$.

claim let $\Gamma:[0,1]^2\to\C^\times$ be continuous. then $\deg(\Gamma|_{\bottom})+\deg(\Gamma|_{\rght})=\deg(\Gamma|_{\lft})+\deg(\Gamma|_{\top})$

proof 1
by reasoning similar to that of paths, we can lift $\Gamma=e^{i\Psi}$ with $\Psi:[0,1]^2\to\C$ continuous. the statement ergo reads $\Psi(1,1)-\Psi(0,0)=\Psi(1,1)-\Psi(0,0)$. ▨


proof 2
first, let us assume $\Gamma|_{\lft}=\Gamma|_{\rght}$ and show $\deg(\Gamma|_{\bottom})=\deg(\Gamma|_{\top})$. fix $\delta=\min \abs{\Gamma}$, and $N$ s.t. $\norm{P-Q}_2\le \frac{1}{N}\implies \abs{\Gamma(P)-\Gamma(Q)} < \delta$. let $\gamma_j=\Gamma|_{[0,1]\times\{j/N\}}$. by the dog walking lemma, $\deg(\gamma_j)=\deg(\gamma_{j+1})$. in total, $\deg(\gamma_0)=\deg(\gamma_N)$ as we wanted.
finally, we may reduce the general case of $\Gamma$ to this one by taking $\Psi(x,y) = \Gamma(xy,x(1-y))\Gamma(x+y(1-x),1-y(1-x))\Gamma(1-x,1-x)$. ▨


proof 3
let $\Delta=\RHS-\LHS$. note that if $[0,1]^2$ is divided into squares $R_{j}$ then $\Delta=\sum_j \Delta_{R_j}$ by cancellation. now, $\Delta_R=0$ if a continuous logarithm exists on $\Gamma(R)$. finally, if $\delta=\min\abs{\Gamma}$ and $N$ is s.t. $\norm{P-Q}_\infty\le 1/N\implies \abs{\Gamma(P)-\Gamma(Q)}\le\delta/2$ then the division of $[0,1]^2$ to $N^2$ subsquares yields $\Delta=0$. ▨

algebraic number theory misc

theorem [Kronecker] a nonzero algebraic integer that lies with its conjugaues inside $|z|\le 1$ has to be a root of unity.

proof
let $\alpha_i$ be the set of conjugates, let $A$ be the companion matrix to their minimal polynomial. then $A$ is similar to $D=\diag(\alpha_i)$. writing $A=PDP\inv$, we have $A^k=PD^kP\inv$. as $|\alpha_i|\le 1$, the entries of $A^k$ are uniformly bounded integers. ergo, by the pigeonhole principle, $A^m=A^{\ell}$ for some $m < \ell$. thus $D^m=D^\ell$, and $\alpha_i^{\ell-m}=1$. ▨

subclaim let $W\le V_n$ be an $\SU(2)$ submodule. if $w=\sum r_j x^{n-j}y^j\in W$ and $r_k\neq 0$ then $x^{n-k}y^k\in W$.

proof
by induction on the number of nonzero coefficients. the base case (one) being immediate. given another nonzero $r_\ell$, the process to follow will kill it : fix $\theta$ some irrational multiple of $\pi$ (so $e^{ir\theta}$ are distinct for $r\in\Z$) and let $g=\left(\begin{array}{cc} e^{i\theta} & 0\\ 0 & e^{i\theta} \end{array}\right)$, and let $w'=gw- e^{(n-2\ell)i\theta}w$. then $w'\in W$ has one less nonzero coefficient (namely the $i\th$). ▨


exercise deduce the claim from the subclaim.

theorem any (finite dimensional, continuous) irreducible representation of $\SU(2)$ is isomorphic to one of the $V_n$.

proof
let $V$ be a representation of $\SU(2)$ and $\chi$ its character. recall the maximal torus $T=\SU(2)\cap\{\text{diagonal}\}\equiv \T=\S^1$. now $\Res_T^{\SU(2)}V$ is a representation of $T$. by the representation theory of $\T$ we have for $t=\left(\begin{array}{cc} \phi & 0\\ 0 & \phi\inv \end{array}\right)\in T$ that $\chi(t)=\sum_{k\in\Z} m_k \phi^k$ for some $m_k\ge0$ (a Laurent polynomial $\phi$). since $t$ is conjugate to $t\inv$, these Laurent polynomials are even ($f(\phi)=f(\phi\inv)$). on one hand $\chi_{V_n}(t)=\disp\sum_{k=0}^n \phi^{n-2k}$ span over $\Z$ the even Laurent polynomials in $\phi$. it follows that for some $r_j\in\Z$ we have $\chi,\sum r_j\chi_{V_n}$ agreeing on $T$. since any conjugacy class of $\SU(2)$ intersects $T$, the are equal. it remains to move the negative coefficients to the side of $\chi$ and note the uniqueness of character decomposition. ▨

set theory basics

exercise any infinite subset $A\sub \N$ is countable.

claim $\N^2$ is countable.

proof
to enumerate the points of the quarter grid $\N^2$, we follow its diagonals. namely the diagonals $x+y=c$ form layers, ordered by $c$. the $c+1$ elements within each layer are ordered by height (namely the $y$ coordinate). in formula, this bijection $\N^2\to \N$ is $(x,y)\mapsto {x+y+1 \choose 2}+y$.
as a second solution, it's easier, and sufficient, to find an injection $f:\N^2\to\N$. one could take $f(a,b)=2^a3^b$. ▨


exercise consider the following game. an invisible monkey lives in $\N$. every second, it jumps further right to a different location. we don't know where the monkey started or how far it can jump, but we do know the jumps are constant. with each passing second, we are allowed to make a guess on the monkey's location. find a procedure that is guaranteed to eventually find the monkey.

claim [Cantor] $2^{\N}$ is uncountable.

proof
we need to show that one cannot enumerate $2^\N$. so, given a sequence $(b_i)_{i\in \N}$ in $2^\N$, we need to construct some $b\in 2^\N$ that differs from all the $b_i$. this can be done as follows : the $i\th$ bit of $b$ will be the $i\th$ bit of $b_i$, flipped. ▨
for obvious geometrical reasons, this method of proof is called diagonalization.


observation consider again the monkey game, only now the monkey starts at the origin, but each second it can choose either to stay in place or move one tile right. now, if the monkey plays well, we won't be able to ever find it, no matter how well we play. from this fact alone we could deduce the uncountability of $2^\N$.

exercise [Cantor] more generally, for any set $X$ we have $|X| < |2^X|$.
